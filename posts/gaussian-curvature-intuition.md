Given a smooth surface $S \subset \mathbb{R}^3$, the **first fundamental form** $I$ measures lengths intrinsically, and the **second fundamental form** $II$ measures how $S$ bends in the ambient space.

The **Gaussian curvature** is

$$K \;=\; \frac{\det II}{\det I}.$$

What's remarkable — Gauss's *Theorema Egregium* — is that $K$ depends only on $I$, even though $II$ is extrinsic. Bend a piece of paper and $K$ stays zero.
